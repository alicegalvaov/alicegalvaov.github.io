The Problem

Most Airbnb hosts set their prices by gut feeling or copying nearby listings. But price is one of the strongest levers they have. Price too high and bookings drop. Price too low and money is left on the table. The real question is: what is the price that actually maximizes yearly profit?

This project set out to answer that by building a machine learning model to predict a listing's occupancy rate, then using that model as an engine for a price optimization curve. If you can predict how often a listing gets booked at any given price, you can find the price that produces the highest yearly revenue.

The project was built as a team for an Applied Machine Learning course, covering data from Los Angeles, Austin, and New York City using publicly available data from Inside Airbnb.

Data

The dataset came from Inside Airbnb, a public platform that scrapes Airbnb listing data. We worked with listings and reviews data across three cities covering the full year of 2024: Los Angeles (45,031 listings), Austin (15,431 listings), and New York City (37,784 listings), totaling over 98,000 unique listings and more than 3 million review records.

Key features included listing price, property type, room type, number of bedrooms and bathrooms, host information (superhost status, response rate, acceptance rate, response time), review scores, location coordinates, and availability windows.

The target variable, Occupancy Rate, was engineered from review data using the Inside Airbnb methodology, which estimates bookings by counting unique review dates per listing across the year. This was one of the most challenging aspects of the project as the proxy measure introduces assumptions, since not every guest leaves a review.

Technical Approach
Step 01
Data Cleaning and Feature Engineering

Removed dollar signs from price fields, standardized property types into broader categories, filtered to the 2024 date range, and applied log transformation to price to handle skewness.

Step 02
Occupancy Rate Calculation

Engineered the target variable by counting unique review dates per listing across 2024, normalized by the maximum possible reviews in the period, following Inside Airbnb's methodology.

Step 03
Preprocessing Pipeline

Built separate pipelines for numeric variables (KNN imputation, Standard Scaler) and categorical variables (most-frequent imputation, One Hot Encoding). Applied a train, validation, test split.

Step 04
Model Training and Selection

Evaluated a baseline Linear Regression, tree-based models, support vector machines, and a deep Neural Network with batch normalization, dropout layers, and a custom R² metric callback.

Step 05
Price Optimization

Used the best model to sweep a price range for individual listings, computing predicted occupancy at each price point and calculating the corresponding yearly profit to find the maximum.

Model Results

We evaluated models on RMSE, MAE, and R². The deep neural network was the best performing model, though the improvements over the baseline were modest, pointing to limitations in the occupancy rate proxy itself.

Price Optimization Output

Using the neural network as the core engine, the optimization function swept a price range from 50% to 800% of the original listing price, predicting occupancy at each point and computing yearly profit. For one example listing in the dataset, the model identified an optimal price of $1,173.50, projecting a maximum yearly profit of $77,992 compared to much less at the original listing price.

The price optimization curve plots predicted profit against price, with the original price marked in red and the recommended price in green, giving hosts a clear visual output they can act on.

Key Findings from EDA
  • Austin has significantly more Superhosts (around 40%) compared to NYC (18%) and LA (21%), suggesting a more engagement-focused hosting community
  • Seasonality matters: both LA and NYC show higher occupancy in January and February, dipping in spring, suggesting hosts should price higher in winter months
  • Location hotspots are real: log price heat maps confirm that Manhattan in NYC, the coastline in LA, and the city center in Austin all command premium pricing
  • Superhost acceptance rates are more consistent than non-superhosts, especially in NYC where non-superhosts show a wide range of low-end outliers
Superhost Distribution by City
NYC has the lowest superhost rate at 34.6%, while LA (48.9%) and Austin (47.6%) are nearly split 50/50, suggesting very different hosting cultures across cities.
Monthly Occupancy Trends Across Cities
Austin and LA peak in February then drop sharply in spring. NYC shows a strong December surge, likely driven by holiday tourism. Seasonality is a key signal for pricing strategy.
Limitations and Honest Reflections

The team approached this project with transparency about its limitations. The occupancy rate proxy assumes every guest leaves a review, which is not true in practice. This ceiling on data quality limited how much any model could improve. The neural network outperformed the baseline, but the gap was modest, and the price optimization curve sometimes suggested unrealistically high optimal prices because the model underweighted the influence of price on bookings.

Future work would focus on acquiring real occupancy data directly from Airbnb, incorporating seasonal pricing features, and exploring reinforcement learning approaches for dynamic pricing optimization.
